    BIOLOGICAL TREATMENT



    The CAS technology is a common wastewater treatment process that uses a combination of aerobic bacteria and aeration to break down organic mater in wastewater. The process involves introducing air into a mixed liquor containing wastewater and microorganisms, which consumes and removes organic pollutants and relies on sludge recirculation to maintain the treatment process.






    Sequencing batch reactor (SBR)


    The SBR technology is a type of wastewater treatment process that uses a single reactor to perform several treatment steps in a timed sequence. SBRs can be used for a range of wastewater treatment applications, including biological nutrient removal and disinfection.






    Rotating Biological Contactor (RBC)


    The RBC treatment process involves pumping wastewater onto rotating disks covered with microorganisms, which form a biofilm on the disks. As the disks rotate, the microorganisms break down and treat the wastewater as it flows over the disks. The treated wastewater is then collected and discharged, while excess biomass is sloughed off the disks and collected for further treatment or disposal. The RBC process is known for its simplicity and low maintenance requirements, and its ability to handle variations in flow rate and organic loading.






    Moving Bed Biofilm Reactor (MBBR)


    The MBBR system uses plastic media with a large surface area to support the growth of microorganisms. The microorganisms atached to the media break down and treat the wastewater as it flows through the reactor. The MBBR process is known for its high treatment efficiency, compact design, and low maintenance requirements.






    SLUDGE TREATMENT



    Sludge is the byproduct of wastewater treatment that consists of a mixture of solids and water. Compared to effluent, sludge contains a significantly higher proportion of solids and thus necessitates specialized treatment processes. If not treated properly, it can result in exceeding discharge limits and potentially result in severe penalties, legal action or system failure. Therefore, managing and treating sludge requires special atention to ensure its safe and effective disposal. Wastewater sludge treatment typically involves several stages to reduce the volume and convert the sludge into a safe and manageable material



    Thickening

    This stage involves concentrating the sludge by removing water to reduce its volume for further treatment.

    Stabilization

    The objective of this stage is to reduce the number of pathogens and reduce the amount of organic matter in the sludge.

    Dewatering

    This stage involves further removing water from the sludge to produce a solid cake that can be easily handled and transported.

    Drying

    This stage further reduces the moisture content of the sludge to create a dry, solid material that can be safely stored or used as a fuel source. There are several methods of drying sludge, including solar drying, thermal drying, and mechanical drying.


    Fat, Oil, & Grease Treatment

    Fat, Oil, and Grease (FOG) treatment is an essential process for managing the wastewater generated by food service establishments and other industries that produce significant amounts of FOG. The treatment process typically involves removing FOG from wastewater through a combination of mechanical separation and chemical treatment. The importance of FOG treatment lies in preventing blockages in sewer lines and wastewater treatment systems, which can lead to overflows, backups, and environmental pollution. Proper FOG treatment not only helps to protect the environment but also ensures compliance with local regulations and reduces the risk of costly fines and penalties.





    Methods for FOG treatment include


    1. Grease traps: mechanical separators that capture FOG from wastewater
    2. Grease interceptors: larger versions of grease traps that can handle higher volumes of wastewater
    3. Biological treatment: using microorganisms to break down FOG
    4. Chemical treatment: adding chemicals to wastewater to break down FOG
    5. Membrane filtration: using membranes to physically separate FOG from wastewater
    6. Coagulation-flocculation: adding chemicals to wastewater to form larger FOG particles that can be more easily removed.
    7. Adsorption: passing wastewater through a bed of clay particles or activated carbon to trap FOG molecules, or allowing FOG molecules to adhere to the surface of clay particles.
